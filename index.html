Assumptions

It is complicated to handle all of a person's budgeting information, and the billion different aspects of a person's financial situation. Because this tool aims to put a date on retirement, all other factors are ignored. If you aren't saving it for retirement, you're spending it.

It is generally accepted that the market will hover around 8% annualized returns. The S&P 500, one of the most widely recognized market indexes, has returned 9.7% yearly over the last 25 years. I tend to err on the side of caution, and have used 7% as the default rate of return from the market.

Currently for simplicity, tax is only applied to your gross income after your 401k contribution. Tax is also a flat 15%, which should cover the majority of households. In the real world, taxes would be applied to 401k withdrawals, and capital gains from after tax investment accounts.

There is also an error on the conservative side of the fence, in that income tax isn't actually flat. Tax is applied at a graduating rate, so that the last dollar you make is taxed at a higher rate than the first portion of the money you make.

See here for a table of US income tax rates, and a brief explanation on how they're applied.

Currently the tool doesn't factor in any social security or pension plans. These will greatly affect real world situations.

A safe withdrawal rate is basically the amount of your portfolio that you can withdraw each year, so that you won't run out of money during your lifetime. In order to last you indefinitely, your safe widthrawal rate should be at or below your rate of return - inflation.

The Trinity Study was conducted to find what would have worked historically, and came to the conclusion that generally 4% is a good fit for a portfolio consisting of stocks and bonds.

If you would like to play with historical withdrawal rates check out FIRECalc. It allows you to play with withdrawal amounts, retirements starting at any point in the past, and different asset allocations.
